process
  • Sampling methods
    Sampling Selecting a specific group of individuals from a larger population to collect data from to represent the general population. Research has more feasibility + lower cost, avoids bias, samples according to a factor (age, gender etc). 50-200 sample size.
    • Random sampling
      Random sampling
      1. selecting respondents: randomised sample from a complete list of identified members within a group (eg. population)
      2. select respondents according to the sample
      3. if respondent rejects, move on to the next respondent on the list

      + Each member of the group has an equal chance of being selected and thus avoids bias

      - unsuitable for very large groups as hard to identify every member

    • Stratified sampling
      Stratified sampling
      1. selecting respondents: every person that passes by from a stratum (category) (eg. age group/social background/race)
      2. stand at a fixed position and select respondents accordingly
      3. reject respondent if not within stratum

      + accurate data as evidence as no irrelevant data is collected

      - hard to identify people within the specified stratum

    • Systematic sampling
      Systematic sampling
      1. selecting respondent: every 3rd person that passes by/ every 3 min
      2. stand at a fixed position and select respondents accordingly
      3. if the respondent rejects, restart from step 1
  • Research question
    • Guiding question
      Guiding question Example: What types of tourism would the Pei Hwa family want to experience once travels are allowed after the pandemic?
    • Hypothesis
      Hypothesis Example: Once travels are allowed, the Pei Hwa family would want to experience Honeypot tourism and Dark tourism the most
  • Data collection
    • Primary data
      Primary data Data collected first-hand through fieldwork. Modes of collection: surveys, observations, experiments, questionnaires, personal interviews. Unnecessary questions should be omitted from questionnaires.
    • Secondary data
      Secondary data Data that have already been collected, compiled and/or published by others for other purposes. Mode of collection: searching online
  • Data representation
    • Line graph
      Line graph
      • relationship/trend of continuous data (increasing or decreasing against time)
      • could be used to estimate future patterns
      • comparative line graphs compare more than one dependent variable to one independent variable to compare the trend of two data sets
      • simple line graphs compare one dependent variable to one independent variable
      • dependent variable y-axis, independent x-axis
    • Bar graphs
      Bar graph
      • patterns of categorical data (more or less)
      • simple bar graphs
      • comparative bar graphs compare two or more data sets per category
      • compound bar graphs subdivide data in a category (eg. age groups)
    • Pie charts
      Pie charts
      • patterns of categorical data
      • easy to interpret
      • size of sectors determined by percentage of data
  • Concluding whether data supports hypothesis
    Format Describe format
    1. overall trend (supported or refuted)
    2. noteworthy examples (supports trend)
    3. exceptions
  • Concluding whether data is valid
    Assessing validity The geographical process is evaluated to determine the reliability and accuracy of the data to conclude its validity. Cannot correct the experiment for when the question asks "how can the experiment be improved?" if the fault is not explicitly stated.

      Accuracy

      Reliability

movement
  • Reasons for movement
    • Convection currents
      Convection currents material in the mantle is heated by core → expands and rises → spreads out beneath the plates → drags plates along → cools and sinks → pulls plates along → sinking material heats up and process repeats
    • Slab-pull force
      Slab-pull force dense, sinking oceanic plate pulls the rest of the plate behind it
  • OC-OC divergence
    OC-OC divergence two plates diverge → tensional forces → fractures at plate boundary → mid-oceanic ridge

    eg. North American plate and European plate diverge to form the mid-atlantic ridge and volcanic islands, Azores.
    • Seafloor spreading
      Seafloor spreading plates further diverge → magma rises through cracks then cools and solidifies → repeat → new ocean floor
    • Volcanic islands
      Volcanic islands magma erupts along the mid-oceanic ridge → cools and solidifies → undersea volcanoes → continue to grow until above sea level
  • CC-CC divergence
    CC-CC divergence two plates diverge → stretched → tensional forces → fracture in rocks → faults → faulting

    eg. African plates, Nubian and Somalian diverges to form the East African Rift System
    • Rift valley
      Rift valley central block of land subside btwn pair of parallel faults → rift valley
    • Block mountains
      Block mountains land masses surrounding block of land subside → block mtn form btwn pair of parallel faults
  • OC-OC convergence
    OC-OC convergence two plates converge and collide → compressional forces → denser plate subduct beneath less dense plate → deep oceanic trench at point of subduction

    eg. Pacific and Philippines plate converge to form the Mariana Islands
    • Volcanoes
      Volcanoes on OC friction → mantle material above melt → magma → rise through cracks cools and solidifies → undersea volcanoes >> chain or arc of islands
    • Earthquakes
      Earthquakes friction between moving rock masses when plate subduct under another → earthquake occur at boundary between two plates
  • CC-CC convergence
    CC-CC convergence two plates converge and collide → compressional forces where crust compressed → immense pressure

    eg. Indian and Eurasian plate colliding to form the Himalayas
    • Range of fold mountains
      Range of fold mountains too thick and buoyant → resist subduction → rocks buckle and fold → massive folding upward or sideways → fold mtns
  • OC-CC convergence
    OC-CC convergence oc plate converge cc plate → compressional forces → denser oc subducts beneath less dense cc → deep oceanic trench at point of subduction

    eg. Australian plate subducts under Eurasian plate, forming the Sunda Trench and Barisan Mountains
    • Volcanoes
      Volcanoes on CC friction → mantle material above melt → magma → rise through cracks cools and solidifies
    • Earthquakes
      Earthquakes friction between moving rock masses when plate subduct under another → earthquake occur at boundary between two plates
    • Fold mountains
      Fold mountains CC too thick and buoyant → resist subduction → rocks buckle and fold → massive folding upward or sideways → fold mtns
  • Transform plate boundaries
    Transform plate boundaries plate slide past each other along transform plate boundary → formation of transform fault → tremendous stress build up at fault
    • Earthquakes

volcanoes
  • Shield volcanoes
    Shield volcanoes
    • occurs on divergent plate boundaries
    • broad summit & gently sloping sides:
      lava flows easily → over longer distance → cools and solidifies → form thin crust → base increases in size over successive eruptions → volcano with gently sloping sides
    • less explosive:
      gas escape easier → less explosive volcanic eruptions
    • low-silica → low viscosity
    • eg. Mount Washington, USA
  • Stratovolcanoes
    Stratovolcanoes
    • occurs on convergent (oceanic) plate boundary
    • narrow summit & steep sloping sides:
      lava flows shorter distance → cools and solidifies → thicker crust over successive eruptions → tall volcano, narrow summit
    • very explosive:
      traps gases more easily → build-up of pressure below earth's surface → magma rises to surface → gas expand → explosion
    • high-silica → high viscosity
    • caldera:
      summit blown off → sides of crater collapse inward due to loss of structural support
    • eg. Mount Vesuvius, Italy
  • Volcanic eruptions
    Volcanic eruptions
    1. magma seeps into the magma chamber, pressure in chamber builds up, volcanic eruption
    2. pyroclasts released from eruption, force depends on pressure built up
    3. lava builds up around the vent, solidifying to form a small volcanic cone called the crater
    4. during formation of volcano, vent may become blocked, forcing magma to find a new exit route to the surface, developing a secondary cone of newer volcanic material
  • Impacts of living in volcanic areas
    • Fertile soil
      Fertile soil lava and ash from volcanic eruptions break down → fertile volcanic soils → agriculture (minerals only after the rocks weathered and broken down for thousands of years)

      eg. volcanic soils of Java, Indonesia support cultivation of various crops like coffee
    • Precious stones and minerals
      Precious stones and minerals volcanic rocks rich in precious stones (can extract after millions of years when rocks erode)

      eg. Volcanic rocks at Kimberly, South Africa one of the world's richest sources of diamond
    • Tourism
      Tourism variety of activities for tourists → hiking, camping / rich in history

      eg. 3 million people visit the ruins of Pompei, Italy after volcanic eruption in 79CE as rich in history
    • Geothermal energy
      Geothermal energy heat in earth's crust in form of hot rocks beneath the surface heats water up → erupts as steam for turbines

      eg. Over 70% of homes in Iceland heated by volcanic steam
    • Destruction and pollution by volcanic materials
      Destruction and pollution by volcanic materials
      • volcanic bombs of heated rocks → fall in surrounding areas → damage to property
        eg. Hawaii 1983 eruption of Kilauea destroyed many homes and highwaysMassive destruction by volcanic materials
      • lava and hot ash → (low-silica) moves rapidly, flows over long distances → damage to large areas
        eg. Eruption of Mt. Pinatubo 1991 emitted 10 million tonnes of lava and 20 million tonnes of sulfur dioxide
      • landslides → structural collapse of volcanic cone during eruption → obstruct flow of rivers, bury villages and farmland
      • ash particles ejected during a volcanic eruption → block sunlight, suffocate crops, cause respiratory problems for people and animals
earthquakes
  • Earthquake
    Earthquake friction between moving plates → slow build-up of stress on rocks along fault line → sudden release of energy when can no longer withstand stress → crust slip many meters
    energy is released in the form of seismic waves from the focus
    stress may cause aftershocks months after the earthquake which may be as powerful

    Epicentre is the point on the surface above the focus
  • Richter scale
    Richter scale measures magnitude of earthquake, 1 increment = 10 times greater magnitude of impact
    • 1.0-2.0: not felt by people but detected by seismograph; described as tremor
    • 2.1-3.0: felt by few people near the epicentre; described as tremor
    • 3.1-4.0: little damage to structures; hanging objects swing
    • 4.1-5.0: minor damage including cracks in walls; felt by many people
    • 5.1-6.0: damage poorly constructed buildings; difficult to walk
    • 6.1-7.0: many structures collapse, cracks in ground; difficult to stand
    • 7.1-8.0: severe damage, most buildings collapse
    • 8.1-9.0: destruction over wide area; landslides common
    • >9.0: destruction over thousands(?) of km of land
  • Extent of damage
    • Magnitude of earthquake
      Magnitude of earthquake Stronger tremors and wider destruction when higher magnitude
      eg. Haiti, 2010 7.0 magnitude earthquake had a death toll of more than 300 000 people.
    • Population density
      Population density Number of people living in affected area: affect fewer people in sparsely than densely populated areas
    • Level of preparedness
      Level of preparedness Amount of preparation, eg. evacuation plans and rescue workers, taken by the authorities and citizens: earthquake is more manageable when prepared
      eg. Tohoku, Japan 2011 9.0 magnitude earthquake had a death toll of 28 000 people.
      eg. Haiti 2010 7.0 magnitude earthquake had a death toll of more than 300 000 people. (lack of preparedness)
    • Distance from epicentre
      Distance from epicentre More severe damage when closer to epicentre

      eg. Christchurch, New Zealand 2011, the earthquake epicentre was in a town a few km away from the city centre, which suffered more damage than areas further.
    • Time of occurrence
      Time of occurrence Time of day an earthquake occurs: determines what people are doing and where they are, affecting chances of survival

      eg. 2400 people died when an earthquake occurred a couple hours after midnight in the Sun Moon Lake Region, Taiwan 1999 → people are sleeping, higher chance of being trapped in house.
    • Type of soil
      Type of soil Where sediments are loose and unconsolidated, seismic waves are amplified → greater damage when earthquakes occur
      Structures on saturated and unconsolidated sediments can be affected by liquefaction when saturated soil flows like liquid

      eg. Christchurch 2011, many houses and buildings had to be abandoned due to liquefaction.
  • Hazards
    • Tsunami
      Tsunami seismic energy from offshore earthquake → forces out a mass of seawater → waves start at height less than 1m, with wavelengths of 100 to 150km at 800km/h → may pass undetected
      reach shallow water → greater friction → slow waves, increase in height → at point of impact, tsunami waves travelling at 30 to 50 km/h and reach height 15m
      (sea recedes minutes before tsunami reaches shore as water rushes to fill in void caused by movement of sea floor)
    • Landslide
      Landslide The rapid downslope movements of soil, rock and vegetation debris.
      ground shaken during earthquake → weaken the slopes of mountains → become unstable

      eg. Peru 1970 earthquake caused landslide on Mount Huascarán, flattening the town of Ranrahirca within seconds
  • Impacts
    • Disruption of services
      Disruption of services vibrations on the ground → snap pipes and break cables services → disrupts supply of electricity, gas and water; may cause outbreak of fires; affects communication services, eg. television broadcasts and telephone connections

      eg. Kobe, Japan 1995 earthquake damaged pipes and transmission lines, disrupting electricity, gas and water supplies to 1of 1.4 million residents.
    • Destruction of property
      Destruction of property widespread destruction of many homes → people may be without homes and have to reside at temporary shelters while their homes are being rebuilt

      eg. Tōhoku, Japan 2011 earthquake caused a large tsunami which resulted in 155000 homes lost.
    • Destruction of infrastructure
      Destruction of infrastructure cracks in infrastructure like roads and bridges → unsafe → disrupts transportation

      eg. After Kobe, Japan 1995 earthquake, many places in the city became difficult to reach.
    • Loss of lives
      Loss of lives Earthquakes and their hazards threaten the lives of those living in earthquake zones

      eg. Tōhoku, Japan 2011 earthquake resulted in 28000 deaths.
responses to earthquakes
  • Preparedness measures
    • Land use regulation
      Land use regulation Set of rules implemented to restrict development in certain vulnerable areas

      eg. California, USA, all new building developments are not built across fault lines or areas at risk of liquefaction.

      • buildings less vulnerable to collapse → less damage → reduce economic loss
      • expensive and may not always work
        eg. Japan's anti-tsunami seawalls were not very effective against the large tsunami waves created by the Tohoku 2011 earthquake.
    • Infrastructure
      Infrastructure Developed with advanced engineering to withstand the vibration associated with an earthquake

      eg. Taipei 101 has dampening devices which act as shock absorbers for seismic activity during earthquakes. Wide base which reduces risk of collapse.

      • buildings less vulnerable to collapse → less damage → reduce economic loss
      • expensive
        eg. Dampening device in Taipei 101 cost US$4 million.
    • Emergency drills
      Emergency drills People practice the steps to take when an earthquake occurs

      eg. Since 1960, Japan conducts emergency drills on Sep 1 for Disaster Prevention Day, during which an earthquake of high magnitude is simulated.

      • awareness of proper response procedures → reduce loss of lives
      • based on the most serious earthquake recorded in the country → not prepared for stronger earthquakes
        eg. Emergency drills did not sufficiently prepare people for Tohoku 2011 earthquake as it was more severe than any previous earthquakes in Japan.
    • Earthquake monitoring and warning systems
      Earthquake monitoring and warning systems Earthquake sensors in earthquake prone zones to monitor vibrations, linked to warning systems to warn people of impending tremors and its severity (duration, frequency, magnitude and location).

      • warned prior to earthquake → evacuate → reduce loss of lives
        eg. Jiuzhaigou, Sichuan, China, earthquake warning systems warned residents 19-40 seconds ahead of the tremors through broadcasting and mobile applications.
      • too severe for warning to have significant impact
        eg. Early warnings for Tohoku, 2011 earthquake were unable to prepare against 30m high tsunami waves which were too big.
    • Tsunami monitoring and warning systems
      Tsunami monitoring and warning systems Pressure sensors in the water monitor pressure and temperature, linked to warning systems to warn people of incoming tsunamis and its severity.

      • warned prior to earthquake → evacuate → reduce loss of lives
        eg. Banda Aceh, Indonesia, 2012, tsunami warning systems released alerts when a magnitude 8.6 earthquake occurred in Northern Indonesia, deploying authorities which helped move people away from the coastline successfully.
      • false alarms → wrongly predict severity
        eg. A tsunami warning for Tohoku 2011 earthquake was issued for a wave of 3m, but the actual were higher.
  • Short-term responses
    • Search and rescue
      Search and rescue People trapped under collapsed buildings are quickly located and freed

      eg. After Tohoku 2011 earthquake, sniffer dogs and heat sensors were used to rescue many who were trapped in 2 towns.
      • victims are rescued → reduce loss of lives
      • limited time of 72 hours before trapped victims die without food and water
        eg. After Tohoku 2011 earthquake, rescue workers only had 3 days to search through 2 towns.
    • Emergency, food and medical supplies
      Emergency, food and medical supplies The injured are treated and clean drinking water is provided to prevent dehydration and spread of diseases.

      eg. After Haiti 2010 earthquake, UN World Food Programme, international aid agencies and the Haitian government worked together to distribute food coupons, allowing redemption of a 25kg bag of rice.

      • victims are nurtured back to health → reduce loss of lives
      • violence and looting when supplies are not sufficient
        eg. After Haiti 2010 earthquake, violence and looting broke out as people fought for food and medical supplies.
  • Long-term responses
    • Rebuilding of infrastructure
      Rebuilding of infrastructure Infrastructure and amenities are rebuilt and improved upon after a disaster.

      • prevent buildings from collapsing for future earthquakes → less damage → reduce economic losses
        eg. After Kobe, Japan, 1985 earthquake, Japan spent billions developing technology for earthquake resistant buildings.
      • earthquake-resistant buildings require additional structures to protect against tsunamis eg. Tsunami by Chilean 2010 earthquake damaged earthquake resistant buildings in coastal areas.
    • Provision of healthcare
      Provision of healthcare Recovery options such as healthcare and long-term counselling are provided as the loss of loved ones, homes or jobs after an earthquake cause long-lasting trauma.

      • problems are identified and addressed early → efficient recovery
        eg. After Christchurch, New Zealand, 2011 earthquake, problems of anxiety and depression were identified and more health workers were deployed to the area.
      • insufficient medical workers, facilities and funds → challenging to establish services eg. After Haiti 2010 earthquake, many survivors continue to lack basic necessities.

  • Climate
    ClimateWeather of a specific region averaged over a long period of time.
    • Equatorial climate
      Equatorial climate
      • 10 degrees north and south of the equator
      • High mean annual temperature: 27°C
      • Small annual temperature range: 2°C
      • High annual rainfall: over 2000mm
      • High relative humidity: over 80%
      • Convectional rain due to high temperatures
      • No distinct wet or dry seasons
    • Monsoon climate
      Monsoon climate
      • Between 5 to 25 degrees north and south of the equator
      • High mean annual temperature but lower than equatorial climate
        (due to concentrated rainfall (from monsoons) during summer which lowers the temperature)
      • High mean monthly temperature before start of wet season: 28°C
      • High mean monthly temperature after start of wet season: 26°C
      • Small annual temperature range of 3 to 4°C
      • Distinct wet and dry seasons
      • High rainfall during wet season: av. 2000mm
      • Much lower rainfall during dry season: 750mm
    • Cool temperate climate
      Cool temperate climate
      • Between 40 to 60 degrees north and south of the equator
      • 4 distinct seasons. Due to tilt of the earth and revolution around the sun
      • Mild winters, cool summers
      • High annual temperature range: 21°C, between -3 and 18°C
      • No distinct wet or dry seasons
      • Relatively low annual rainfall: between 300mm and 900mm

  • Influences
    • Latitude
      Latitude Distance in degrees of any point on the earth measured north or south from the equator, shown by horizontal lines, eg. equatorial line is at 0°; 1°22'N 1 degree and 22 minutes north of the equator.
      Affects annual temperature range
      Most important factor affecting temperature

      Solar angle is the angle between the tangent and the sun's ray at the point of incidence

      Temperature changes
      • higher latitudes: solar angle is lower → solar energy spread out over a larger area → lower annual temp range
      • lower latitudes: solar angle is high → heat from the sun is concentrated on a small area → higher annual temp range
      • certain tilt of the earth → north pole completely exposed & south pole completely shielded from the sun's rays → months of sunlight (summer) in northern hemisphere & months of darkness (winter) in southern hemisphere
    • Altitude
      Altitude Height of a place in relation to sea level.
      6.5°C decrease per 1000m increase in altitude (atmosphere is mostly heated by the earth's surface)

      solar energy enters atmosphere as shortwave radiation & reaches earth's surface → warm surface emits heat as longwave radiation → absorbed by greenhouse gases → warms the atmosphere (greenhouse effect)
      increasing altitude → further from surface → less longwave radiation → decreasing temperature
    • Cloud cover
      Cloud cover Presence of clouds: small diurnal temperature range
      • day: clouds reflect large amount of solar radiation back to space & absorbs surface radiation → cooler surface
      • night: clouds absorb more surface radiation & prevents it from escaping into space → warmer surface
      Absence of clouds: higher diurnal temperature range
      • day: large amount of solar radiation reach the surface → surface heats up fast → warmer surface
      • night: more surface radiation escapes into space → cooler surface
    • Distance from the sea
      Effect of distance from the sea Coastal areas smaller annual temperature range than inland areas.
      Maritime effect: effect of sea on temperature
      • summer: sea heats up slower → air over sea cooler than land → temperature of coastal areas lower than land
      • winter: sea cools down slower → air over sea warmer than land → temperature of coastal areas higher than land
      Continental effect: effect of land on temperature
      • inland areas are further from the sea → temperatures are not influenced by the sea → inland areas experience warmer summers and colder winters → larger annual temperature range on land
rainfall
  • Relative humidity
    Relative humidity total/maximum moisture * 100%
    warmer → amount of water vapour stays the same & air expands → able to hold more water → lower RH

    saturated air:
    • holds the maximum amount of water
    • relative humidity is 100%
    • occurs at dew point temperature
    • condensation occurs
  • Clouds
    Clouds Visible mass of water droplets or ice crystals that are suspended in the atmosphere.
    1. earth's surface heats up
    2. liquid water evaporates to form water vapour
    3. water vapour (less dense than air) rises and warm air expands and rises
    4. higher up the atmosphere, air is cooler
    5. at dew point temperature, air is saturated and vapour condenses into droplets on tiny particles like dust called the condensation nuclei
    6. droplets coalesce and become larger to form clouds
  • Convectional rain
    Convectional rain intense rain, lightning and thunder, short time
    1. earth's surface heated intensely
    2. liquid water evaporates to form water vapour
    3. water vapour (less dense than air) rises and warm air expands and rises
    4. higher up the atmosphere, air is cooler
    5. at dew point temperature, air is saturated and vapour condenses to form cumulonimbus clouds
    6. when water droplets become large and heavy enough, they fall to the ground as rain
  • Relief rain
    Relief rain
    1. prevailing wind forces warm, moist air to rise over the windward side of a landform such as a mountain range
    2. higher up the atmosphere, air is cooler
    3. at dew point temperature, vapour condenses to form clouds
    4. when water droplets in the clouds become large and heavy enough, they fall to the ground as rain on the windward side as the clouds move to the leeward side
    5. when the clouds reach the leeward side, no rain falls and air is dry
air
  • Air pressure
    Air pressure The force exerted on a unit area of earth's surface by the weight of the air column above it.
    Decreases as altitude increases
    Decreases as temperature increases
  • Wind
    Wind Movement of air from an area with high pressure to low pressure.
    Pressure gradient: difference in pressure between two locations
    Higher pressure gradient, higher wind speed.

    Components
    • speed (anemometer) represented with beaufort scale
    • direction (wind vane)
    • frequency (wind rose)
  • Breezes
    • Land
      Land breeze Land loses heat and cools down faster than water bodies at night.
      Land (higher air pressure) to sea (lower air pressure)
    • Sea
      Sea breeze Land gains heat and heats up faster than water bodies at night.
      Sea (higher air pressure) to land (lower air pressure)
  • Monsoon winds
    • Southwest
      Southwest monsoon winds between June and September,
      (summer in the northern hemisphere) air over Central Asia heats up, expands and rises → region of low pressure
      (winter in southern hemisphere) air over Australia cools, shrinks and sinks → region of high pressure

      air from Australia moves to the Indian sub-continent and Central Asia as the southeast ⬉ monsoon winds → cross the equator, Coriolis Effect deflects winds to the right → southwest ⬈ monsoon winds
    • Northeast
      Northeast monsoon winds between October and February,
      (summer in the southern hemisphere) air over Australia heats up, expands and rises → region of low pressure
      (winter in northern hemisphere) air over Central Asia and the Indian Subcontinent cool, shrinks and sinks → forming a region of high pressure

      air from Central Asia and Indian sub-continent moves to Australia as the northwest ⬊ monsoon winds → cross the Equator, Coriolis Effect deflects winds to the left → northeast ⬋ monsoon winds.
climate change
  • Greenhouse effect
    Greenhouse effect greenhouse effect: natural greenhouse gases trap heat in the atmosphere which keeps earth's surface warm to sustain life.
    enhanced greenhouse effect: human activities increase concentration of greenhouse gases → traps more heat → increased temperatures

    solar energy enters atmosphere as shortwave radiation → some is reflected but most is absorbed by earth's surface → warm surface emits heat as longwave radiation → absorbed by greenhouse gases → warms the atmosphere (greenhouse effect)
  • Natural causes
    • Variations in solar output
      Variations in solar output sunspots: dark spots, cooler regions of sun's surface

      changes in magnetic activity → sunspots increase with intense solar magnetic activity → areas surrounding sunspots radiate more energy to make up for lower temperatures of sunspot areas → global temperatures rise → variations in annual temperatures
    • Volcanic eruptions
      Volcanic eruptions volcanic eruption releases large volume of carbon dioxide, water, sulfur dioxide, dust and ash → sulfur dioxide reacts with water to form sulfuric acid → sulfur-based particles, water, dust and ash reflect sunlight → cooling effect

      eg. Eruption of Mt. Pinatubo 1991 released 17 million tonnes of sulfur dioxide and lowered global temperatures for 2 years.
  • Anthropogenic causes
    • Deforestation
      Deforestation removal of trees → fewer trees to take in carbon dioxide during photosynthesis → higher concentration of carbon dioxide (greenhouse gas) → enhanced greenhouse effect (trap more heat) → global warming

      eg. Between 2010 and 2015, 52000km2 of rainforest was lost every year.
    • Agriculturalisation
      Agriculturalisation cultivation of livestock and plants → emission of methane from livestock, nitrous oxide from fertiliser & carbon dioxide (greenhouse gas) → enhanced greenhouse effect → global warming

      eg. Argentina, methane from cows accounts for 30% of the country's greenhouse gas emissions.
    • Industrialisation
      Industrialisation production of goods and services → consumption of fossil fuels → emission of carbon dioxide (greenhouse gas) → enhanced greenhouse effect → global warming

      eg. Manufacturing a mobile phone releases 60kg of CO2 while manufacturing a computer and a monitor releases 275kg of CO2.
    • Urbanisation
      Urbanisation development & population of urban areas → increase in agricultural, industrial, commercial and domestic practices → higher emission of greenhouse gases → enhanced greenhouse effect → global warming
  • Impacts
    • Sea level rise
      Sea level rise The mean height of the sea's surface between high tide and low tide relative to land

      higher temperature → melting of glaciers in Greenland and Antarctica & water in sea expand → rise in sea level
      threaten low-lying areas where people live

      eg. Majuro Atoll in the Pacific Ocean will lose 80% of its land if sea level rises by 0.5m.
    • More frequent extreme weather events
      More frequent extreme weather events higher land and sea surface temperatures → higher water vapour and latent heat in a warmer atmosphere → powerful driving force for extreme weather events (heatwave)
      put vital organs at risk & heat-exhaustion when unable to cool body sufficiently

      eg. August 2003, Europe experienced a heat wave that killed more than 70,000 people.
    • Spread of insect-borne diseases
      Spread of insect-borne diseases Disease transmitted to humans and animals by insect

      higher temperature & more rainfall in various parts of the world → facilitate insect (mosquitoe) breeding
      spread of insect-borne diseases (dengue fever/malaria) → causes illnesses that may be lethal

      eg. 2004, Nepal and Bhutan, countries which have cool temperate climates, experienced their first cases of dengue due to rise in temperatures.
    • Lengthened growing seasons
      Lengthened growing seasons higher temperature → more favourable climates for certain crops → longer growing seasons
  • Responses
    • International agreements
      International agreements Contracts signed by two or more countries in cooperation to combat climate change

      eg. Kyoto Protocol initiated the support of many countries to come up with measures to address climate change and put them into practice & target set for countries to reduce greenhouse gas emissions by at least 5% below 1990 levels.

      • increased participation of countries to reduce greenhouse gas emissions → reduced enhanced greenhouse effect → reduced global warming
        eg. targets met or exceeded by many countries, including Finland, Greece and Ireland
      • eg. persisting high greenhouse gas emissions from countries that did not sign the agreement, including India, USA, China
    • National responses
      National responses Implementation of policies by individual countries to combat climate change

      eg. Singapore Green Plan 2012 in Singapore aimed for natural gas, which is a cleaner form of energy than coal as it does not produce smoke which contains greenhouse gases, to generate 60% of Singapore’s energy needs by 2012

      • individual efforts to reduce in greenhouse gas emissions → reduced enhanced greenhouse effect → reduced global warming
        eg. notable improvements as 79% was achieved as early as 2010
      • eg. requires complex treatment plants to process and pipelines to transport which are expensive due to high maintenance BUT Singapore is an affluent country and can afford the higher costs

promoting tourism
  • Government
    Government What: authorise air landings, devising strategies to help promote tourism, planning, funding and building infrastructure projects
    • influence number of visitors and hotels
    • biased and unreliable → want to promote tourism, exaggerating only the positives

    eg. In 1980s, Singapore Tourism Promotion Board (STPB) funded S$1 billion Tourism Product Development Plan which called for the preservation of Singapore's historic districts such as Chinatown and Little India
  • Media
    Media What: reports about country or area through TV, radio, news, Internet
    • influence a wider audience, effective
    • biased and unreliable → promoted by companies, exaggerating only the positives

    eg. Singapore's news channel, CNA, delivers up-to-date information to the Asia-Pacific region, including the state of tourism locally and internationally
  • International organisations
    International organisations What: group of countries working together to send out information and reports about a place.
    • reliable → data, positives and negatives & more resources generated by different countries
    • too technical for some people to understand

    eg. World Health Organisation (WHO) had provided situation updates and medical advise on the avian influenza (aka H5N1) from 2003 to 2013
growth factors
  • Technological advancements
    • Better and affordable transport
      Improvements in transport developments in transport technology have led to improvements in safety, shorter travelling time & more affordability on average
      enabled people to travel further and more frequently, internationally, including destinations not covered by major commercial airlines like the Bhutan, the Pacific Islands

      eg. July 1949, the De Havilland Comet 1 took its first flight. The plane took 4 days to travel from Singapore to London. April 2005, Airbus A380 could travel from Singapore to London in 14 hours
    • Ease of access to information
      More accessible information information more efficiently distributed (via internet) → more updated and accessible
      able to view recommendations for travel destinations globally → help international tourists find destination suitable for them → increased interest in travelling

      eg. Tripadvisor is an online travel platform that provides readily available reviews about millions of accommodations, restaurants, experiences, airlines, and cruises globally
  • Changing demand
    • Disposable income
      Disposable income amount of income left to an individual after taxes have been paid that can be used for saving or spending
      spend on things that will improve quality of life, including travelling for leisure
      BUT the Internet is more significant in terms of affecting more countries

      eg. China experiencing rapid economic growth
    • Leisure time
      Leisure time time when people are free from work responsibilities
      able to dedicate time for travelling

      eg. Australia, many employees can exchange paid overtime work for leave
    • Changing lifestyle
      Changing lifestyle the change in way people live and work, from generation to generation
      life expectancy is increasing due to better medical technology
      more time after retirement to travel

      eg. retirees like grey nomads in Australia travel in mobile homes to explore remote parts of the country
  • Destination attributes
    • Quality of attraction
      Quality of attraction feature or quality that makes a place interesting or enjoyable
      more interesting attractions will make a place more unique → attract more tourists

      eg. Dubai 2017, there were more than 15 million tourists. One of the most popular attraction is the Burj Al Arab luxury hotel built on an artificial island
    • Quality of infrastructure and services
      Quality of infrastructure and services transport networks, communication networks, electrical frameworks, water, and waste disposal systems
      more access to essential facilities → travelling more comfortable → more willing to visit
      higher capacity and logistics efficiency → allow more tourists to visit

      eg. Changi airport, Singapore, rated as one of the best airports in the world, has high capacity and safety and security standards
    • Access to information
      Access to information ease with which information can be obtained
      tourists who need access to travel-related information can obtain it → able to visit a destination

      eg. Singapore, many signs translated to multiple languages like English, Chinese, Malay, and Tamil which allow tourists to understand these signs if they speak at least one of these languages
decline factors
  • Disasters
    Disasters event that cause a great deal of damage to properties, lead to injuries or cause loss of lives
    risk to the safety of tourist & damage to essential tourist infrastructure & reputation of being unsafe → discouraged from visiting
    recuperating emotionally, economically or physically from damages of personal property, injury or loss → discouraged from travelling

    eg. By end of 2011, Japan's international tourist arrivals decreased by 28% to 6.2 million people due to Tohoku earthquake, March 2011
  • Recessions
    Recessions general slowdown in economic activities (global and regional)
    loss of income or jobs → cut back on spending → discouraged from spending on travel internationally & increase in domestic travel

    eg. The 2008 Global Financial Crisis backrupted some of the world's largest financial firms like the Lehman Brothers. Economies in many countries slowed down.
  • Political situations
    Political situations state on disagreement between different groups of people that may result in wars (terrorist attack/war)
    risk to the safety of tourist & damage to essential tourist infrastructure & damaged reputation → discouraged from visiting

    eg. Between March and October in 2011, governments of most countries banned their citizens from visiting Libya due to a civil war in the country
  • Diseases
    Diseases outbreak of diseases: sudden widespread occurrence of disease in an area
    risk spreading/contracting disease by travelling & regulations by government for travelling domestically and internationally → drop in tourism

    eg. COVID-19 caused international travel to plunge by 72% in 2020, the worst year on record for tourism, putting the number of travellers back to levels of 30 years ago
impacts
  • Economic
    • Employment opportunities
      Employment opportunities (by people) work performed for a wage or salary
      increase in tourism-related jobs (tour guides and hotel staff)

      eg. 2011, the tourism industry employed over 235 million people worldwide, roughly 6 to 8% of all the jobs in the world.
    • Growth in income
      Growth in income growth in income for individuals and the country → improves economy of country

      eg. 2011, worldwide tourism receipts exceeded US$1 trillion, generating large revenue for countries
    • Development in infrastructure and facilities
      Development in infrastructure and facilities transport networks, communication networks, electrical frameworks, water, and waste disposal systems
      built to enhance tourism also allows better access to markets, healthcare, education, and jobs → benefit locals

      eg. Beijing, China, expanded underground systems that were used to move large number of visitors during the Summer Olympic Games in 2008 continue to serve local populations
    • Seasonal unemployment
      Seasonal unemployment certain tourism activities depend on climatic conditions → fluctuation in tourist numbers throughout the year → less income on the months with low tourists

      eg. City of Sapporo, Japan, receives many visitors from Dec-Feb as visitors want to engage in winter sports such as skiing
    • Seasonal under-use of facilities
      Seasonal under-use of facilities certain tourism activities depend on climatic conditions → facilities built specifically for certain events under-used when event is over → govt fund maintenance → costly

      eg. China, 2011, only one-third of major sports venues have managed to break even.
    • Shortage of resources
      Shortage of resources tourist infrastructure require use of large amounts of land, water and power to meet the needs of tourists → places and people around facilities are neglected

      eg. Bali, 2012, golf courses used 3mil litres of water every day while 1.7mil of the island's 3.9mil residents had inadequate access to clean water
  • Socio-cultural
    • Preservation of local customs and heritage
      Preservation of local customs and heritage the way people live; economic activities, traditional beliefs, and religious practices
      (historical sites) preserved to make it more attractive to tourists & revenue generated from tourism funds preservation

      eg. Siem Reap, Cambodia, Angkor Wat complex has entry fees that help fund conservation efforts
    • Dilution of local customs and heritage
      Dilution of local customs and heritage weakening of locals' respect to the predominating significance and identity of their cultural tradition
      cultural practices modified to meet demands and expectations of tourists for profit → serves a different purpose → authenticity is lost

      eg. Thailand, tourists treat may the Kayan Lahwi women as exhibits as they have to pay a fee to enter their village, their neck rings become for show to gain profits instead of to preserve their cultural traditions
    • Increased crime
      Increased crime popular tourism sites → tourists prone to being mugged at their hotels, ATMs or tourist attractions → unhappy & conflict
      tourists not on guard on holiday → scammed by goods and services sold at greatly inflated prices → unhappy & conflict

      eg. The Louvre closed in April 2013 as staff protested against the increasing problem of pickpockets there
  • Environmental
    • Conservation of natural environment
      Conservation of natural environment funding from tourism can be used to conserve vulnerable environments & natural environments created or maintained to attract tourists

      eg. Sungei Buloh Wetland Reserve, Singapore, home to the world's rarest mangroves, is also a tourism destination
    • Vandalism
      Vandalism deliberately damaging property belonging to other people

      eg. The thousands of stones of the Great Wall of China are covered with graffiti
    • Littering and pollution
      Littering and pollution poor measures to properly manage waste → excessive litter produced by tourists → area becomes toxic to wildlife and degrades

      eg. Many islands in the Caribbean have limited space on land to treat waste from ships, thus waste is sometimes dumped into the Caribbean sea by ships, causing pollution
    • Destruction of habitats
      Destruction of habitats tourists may trample on plants, collect eggs and feathers of birds as souvenirs, make too much noise which can disturb and frighten off animals → ruin the habitats of animals

      eg. Egypt's Red Sea coast, a major snorkelling destination, have its habitats of coral reefs and exotic fish damaged by tourists who collect corals for souvenirs
    • Carbon footprint
      Carbon footprint amount of greenhouse gas emissions produced by activities that involve use of fossil fuels
      more tourists travelling → higher amount of greenhouse gas being emitted from vehicles

      eg. Tourism is responsible for roughly 8% of the world's carbon emissions in 2018, with aviation contributing 2.4%
    • Increased congestion
      Increased congestion large number of tourists cause overcrowding in areas close to popular attractions → vehicular and pedestrian traffic becomes congested → decreased efficiency of transport networks

      eg. Rome, Italy, many older walkways and narrow streets are filled with tourists and the huge inflow of vehicles contributes to traffic
  • Conservation and sustainability
    Conservation & sustainability Conservation: the careful management and use of resources
    • laws and regulations (fines)
    • support from local people
    • minimising leakages from tourist revenues

    Sustainable tourism: tourism organised in a way that allows it to continue without causing damage to the environment or society and culture while addressing the needs of tourists.
    • laws and regulations (entrance fees and taxes)
tensions
  • Tensions between tourists and locals
    How tourists affect locals Needs of tourists and locals may conflict with needs of locals

    eg. Bali, Indonesia, locals in Bali object to public display of affection, which some tourists may perceive as normal
  • Tensions between tourists and the environment
    How tourists damage the environment Needs of tourists may conflict with need to conserve the environment

    eg. Machu Picchu, Peru, draws on average 3300 visitors every day, causing trails with rare indigenous plant and wildlife to become eroded and polluted with rubbish by tourists
responsibilities
  • Local communities
    Local communities A group of people living in the same territory through community-based tourism
    they can conserve tourist areas and benefit economically (done in partnership with a government or NGO)

    eg. Candirejo Village, Indonesia, villagers, with support from the government, cooperated to manage the community's tourism-related programmes, increasing income per villager by 12.5% from 2001 to 2003
  • Visitors
    Visitors responsibility to respect both the environment and the local population of the place they are visiting.

    eg. Visitors, out of respect, should be dressed appropriately to visit temples, such as Wat Benchamabophit, a temple in Bangkok, Thailand
  • Tour operators
    Tour operators May belong to associations that are increasingly concerned with conserving and protecting environments visited by tourists for sustainability

    eg. 2006, Phuket Alternative Tours (PAT) was set up by a team of tour operators in Phuket. Tour operators who want to work under PAT have to sign the Environmental and Cultural Code of Practice which commits members to: operate in an environmentally sustainable way, seek to enhance the natural environment, and create awareness about environmental conservation for visitors
  • Planning Authorities
    Planning Authorities Have influence → determine the future quality of environments by controlling how many visitors can visit a site, allocating space for infrastructure such as roads and hotels, and funding of site preservation

    eg. In 1980s, Singapore Tourism Promotion Board (STPB) funded S$1 billion Tourism Product Development Plan which called for the preservation of Singapore's historic districts such as Chinatown and Little India
  • Non-governmental organisations
    NGOs Non-profit organisations operating independently of governments that may be concerned with conserving and protecting environments visited by tourists for sustainability

    eg. Since 1990s, The International Ecotourism Society (TIES) has developed guidelines, conducted training courses, provided technical assistance and published research papers related to sustainable tourism.

consumption patterns factors
  • Economic
    • Disposable income
      Disposable income amount of income left to an individual after taxes have been paid that can be used for saving or spending

      DCs:
      • generally high because have jobs
      • type of food differs; eat more expensive food like organic food, olive oil over cooking oil & more meat and less cereal
      LDCs:
      • generally low, exponentially increasing in rapidly developing LDCs (China/India)
      • type of food differs; more meat, less cereal (sign of affluency)
      • eg. Taiwan, high economic growth, rice consumption declines by half, meat consumption increase 4x
    • Pricing
      Pricing eg. 2006-2008 food crisis increased price of cereals by up to 300%, pushing more than 100 million people worldwide into chronic hunger and poverty.

      DCs:
      • type of food differs; select less expensive options
      LDCs:
      • higher influence; lower disposable income in LDCs → proportion of income spent on food is higher for LDCs than DCs → LDCs more affected than DCs
      • affects total food consumed; less able to afford
  • Socio-cultural
    • Food preferences
      Food preferences people's concern regarding health, moral principles, income, and the environment influence their choices about the food they consume
      eg. Buddhists consume a more vegetarian diet because believe in not harming animals, even if disposable income increases, meat consumption still low.

      DCs:
      • type of food differs; health concerns → diets include more organic foods (less chemicals)
      LDCs:
      • mainly rapidly developing LDCs (China/India)
      • type of food differs; fast food (KFC) is affordable, prepared quickly and widespread → more accessible (ie. less driving)
    • Population growth
      Population growth affects total food consumption

      DCs:
      • lower influence; more educated → more family planning
      LDCs:
      • higher influence; less educated → lack of family planning & need for labour → population growth increases rapidly
  • Political
    • Stability of food supply
      Stability of food supply stable when safe and nutritious food is available to people at all times
      affected by threats (civil wars/natural disasters) → hinders local food production → require governments to take action to increase food supply by increasing food production or imports
      eg. Libya, April 2011 civil war caused food stocks in the country to rapidly deplete and were not being replenished.

      DCs:
      • lower influence; more advanced technology & wealthy → can increase food production & import quickly → higher ability to increase the food supply
      • type of food differs; import different food
      LDCs:
      • higher influence; lower access to technology & not wealthy → lower ability to produce & import food → lower ability to increase the food supply
      • affects total food consumption; low food supply
    • Food safety
      Food safety guidelines which ensure proper handling, preparation, transportation, and storage of food prevent foodborne diseases so that food is safe for consumption (responsibility of the government)
      type of food differs
      eg. Fukushima, Japan, 2011 tsunami triggered a nuclear disaster and seafood was contaminated with nuclear waste, government is responsible to inform the public.
